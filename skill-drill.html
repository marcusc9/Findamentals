Build the model so the decision can survive the spreadsheet.

A good financial model is not a tidy Excel file. It is a decision system: clear inputs, transparent assumptions, copy-across calculations, useful outputs, checks, scenarios, and an audit trail strong enough to challenge the answer.

Model architecture Checks Scenarios
Inputs, calculations, outputs, checks, scenarios, audit trail.

Start with architecture before formulas. A compact operating model for a UK subscription software business might begin with opening customers, churn, new customer additions, average revenue per account, gross margin, fixed staff costs, marketing spend, and a working-capital lag. Those inputs should be visibly separate from calculations and outputs.

The calculation engine then turns the inputs into revenue, gross profit, EBITDA, free cash flow, runway, and debt capacity. The core equations are plain: revenue = volume x price; EBITDA = revenue - variable costs - fixed costs; closing debt = opening debt + drawdowns - repayments. If the model needs a paragraph to explain a formula, the formula probably needs to be broken apart.

Use functions to make the decision clearer, not to make the workbook clever. XLOOKUP or INDEX/MATCH can pull scenario assumptions without hardcoded row references. SUMIFS can aggregate revenue or cost lines by segment. EOMONTH keeps time columns consistent. IF or IFS can drive flags. CHOOSE can switch scenarios. LET can make a longer calculation readable when it avoids repeating the same expression.

The controls are as important as the forecast. Add a balance-sheet check, cash-flow tie-out, sign-convention check, hardcode detection, circularity warning, formula consistency test, and sensitivity sanity check. Short, copy-across formulas reduce risk because reviewers can compare the same logic across periods. Consistent time columns reduce the chance that one month, quarter, or year has silently become a special case.

The final model should make stress testing easy. Lower new-customer growth, raise churn, delay the price increase, let fixed costs creep, or stretch working capital. If one switch can show the effect on EBITDA, free cash flow, runway, and debt capacity, the spreadsheet is doing its real job: helping the decision survive contact with uncertainty.
