The junk-bond lesson: private credit is new plumbing for an old credit-cycle problem.

The late-1980s junk-bond boom is not the same market as today's private credit. It is a useful comparison because both show how a financing innovation can solve a real problem before investor demand, leverage, and underwriting discipline start to stretch.

The instrument changes. The credit-cycle pressure points rhyme.

The useful lesson from the Drexel and Milken era is not that private credit is simply junk bonds in another wrapper. The markets are different. Private credit loans are often directly negotiated, floating-rate, institutionally held, and less exposed to daily public-market marks than traded high-yield bonds.

The rhyme is in the cycle. Credit innovation often begins by solving a real financing problem: borrowers need capital, traditional lenders are constrained, and investors want higher income. The danger arrives when demand for the product encourages more aggressive assumptions, weaker protections, higher leverage, and confidence that refinancing will always be available.

In the junk-bond cycle, price discovery was public and sometimes brutal. In private credit, price discovery can be delayed. A loan may not trade often, the lender may have room to renegotiate, and the mark may move more slowly than sentiment. That can be a stabiliser when the borrower is fundamentally sound. It can also delay recognition when the loan needs a new story to avoid being marked down.

The comparison also clarifies what not to overstate. Private credit is not automatically a public high-yield replay. It has different holders, different documentation, different liquidity, and often closer lender-borrower relationships. Those differences can help manage stress. They can also make the market harder for outsiders to read.

The practical lesson is that opacity is not the same thing as resilience. If refinancing becomes harder, leverage is high, documentation is loose, and investor demand cools, private credit can still face the old credit-cycle problem: confidence moves before every loss is visible. The instrument is newer, but the discipline needed to survive the cycle is familiar.
