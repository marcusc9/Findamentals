The MPC should hold, but prepare the cut.

The recommendation for the Bank of England is a cautious hold at the next meeting, paired with a clearer map for when cuts can resume. The Middle East energy shock has lifted near-term inflation risk, but the softer labour market argues against turning one supply shock into a new tightening campaign.

Recommendation memo 3.75% Bank Rate Bank of England
Hold now. Define the data threshold for easing next.

The MPC should hold Bank Rate at 3.75 per cent for now. That is not the same as saying the committee should sound hawkish. The better recommendation is to treat the latest energy shock as a serious inflation risk, keep policy steady while the first-round effects arrive, and explain exactly what would justify restarting cuts once the medium-term picture is clearer.

The case for holding starts with the Bank’s March minutes. The committee voted unanimously to keep Bank Rate unchanged after the conflict in the Middle East pushed up global energy and commodity prices. The danger is not that monetary policy can lower oil prices. It cannot. The danger is that fuel, utility, transport, food, and business input costs become second- round wage and price-setting behaviour.

The case against tightening is just as important. ONS labour-market data show vacancies falling to their lowest level since early 2021 and regular pay growth slowing to 3.6 per cent. Real pay is barely positive. PwC’s March PMI commentary made the trade-off plain: demand has weakened, cost pressures have picked up, and the prospect of near-term cuts has faded. This is not a clean overheating story.

The practical recommendation is therefore conditional. Hold for now. Make clear that a larger or more persistent energy shock could still require a firmer stance. But also say, plainly, that if the shock fades, inflation expectations stay contained, and labour-market slack widens, cuts should resume before policy starts doing unnecessary damage. The MPC’s job is not to punish the economy for imported energy prices. It is to stop those prices from hardening into domestic inflation behaviour.

For markets, the point is less a single meeting than the reaction function behind it. Sterling, gilts, mortgages, and business cashflow all depend on a Bank that can distinguish credibility from rigidity. The cleaner line is to hold while uncertainty is highest, prepare the cut when second-round effects fail to show up, and explain the threshold in advance.
