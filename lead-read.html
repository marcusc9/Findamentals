Can AI capex still clear the return-on-capital test?

The next AI earnings test is no longer adoption hype. It is capital discipline: whether Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta can turn data-centre spend into durable revenue, margins, and returns before depreciation and funding costs catch up.

The AI trade now has to answer like a capital project.

The clean way to read the hyperscaler earnings cluster is to stop asking whether AI demand exists and start asking whether the marginal dollar of AI capex is earning its keep. A useful framework separates maintenance capex from growth capex, then tests the growth spend against incremental cloud revenue, model monetisation, depreciation, power costs, operating leverage, and balance-sheet capacity.

Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta all face versions of the same investor question. If data-centre spend is pulling forward demand that will convert into higher cloud usage, enterprise AI tools, advertising efficiency, or platform engagement, then the build-out can still be rational. If the spend mostly raises the asset base while pricing power and utilisation lag, the return-on-capital story gets thinner.

The first check is revenue quality. AI revenue should be separated into direct monetisation, cloud consumption, retention benefits, and efficiency gains. A customer using more compute is not the same as a customer paying enough to cover accelerators, networking, power, land, cooling, software, and support. The second check is margin funding: whether layoffs, procurement discipline, and existing operating leverage are genuinely absorbing the build, or merely hiding the early cost curve.

The third check is depreciation. A company can report strong demand while the economics are still being pulled down by shorter useful lives, faster refresh cycles, and under-used capacity. Investors should watch whether management talks about utilisation, inference pricing, customer commitments, and asset lives with enough precision to support the capex path rather than simply repeating that demand is high.

Finally, connect the equity story to the debt story. The OECD has warned that AI investment is becoming material for credit markets because the sector's bond issuance and capital expenditure are no longer small side details. That makes the earnings call a financing test as much as a product test. The strongest AI narrative is the one that can show the return bridge: spend, utilisation, revenue, margin, cash conversion, and funding resilience.
