Historical Lens • 26 April 2026

Inflation memory, not inflation nostalgia.

Historical comparison is most useful when it sharpens judgement rather than forces a perfect analogy. The older inflation episodes remind us that credibility, expectations, and timing matter. They do not tell us that this cycle must unfold exactly the same way.

History works best as a filter, not a script.

The first lesson from the late 1970s is that inflation becomes harder to control when the public doubts whether policymakers will truly lean against it. Federal Reserve history on the Great Inflation and Volcker’s 1979 shift makes that credibility problem impossible to miss. Once expectations become unanchored, restoring trust is costly.

The second lesson from the early 1980s is that beating inflation can create collateral damage, especially in interest-sensitive and balance-sheet-heavy parts of the economy. The point is not to dramatise every modern tightening cycle as a rerun of Volcker. It is to remember that rate policy works through real institutions, financing structures, and labour markets. Somebody always feels the transmission first.

The third lesson comes from later easing cycles. A 2024 Federal Reserve note looking across past episodes argues that successful easing usually begins after inflation is moving down, but before it has fully returned to target. That nuance matters now. Markets often behave as if the only question is the timing of the first cut. History suggests the more important question is whether disinflation is convincing enough to let easing begin without reigniting doubt.

So the right historical posture is neither panic nor amnesia. Use the past to recognise patterns: credibility matters, second-round effects matter, and policy relief is safest when the inflation trend is genuinely improving. What history gives you is not certainty. It gives you a better filter.
