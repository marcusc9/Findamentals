Black Wednesday and the cost of credibility.

On 16 September 1992, the UK tried to defend sterling inside the Exchange Rate Mechanism and discovered that market pressure can overpower an official line when the economics no longer fit. The lesson is not that every currency wobble becomes a crisis. It is that credibility has to be attached to a policy regime the economy can bear.

A broken anchor can be more expensive than an honest reset.

Britain entered the ERM in 1990 with sterling tied to a permitted band against other European currencies. The political promise was discipline: a stable exchange-rate anchor would import anti-inflation credibility and help prepare the UK for deeper European monetary integration. The economic problem was that the chosen level of sterling became harder to defend as the UK economy weakened and German interest-rate pressure shaped the system.

On Black Wednesday, the government and Bank of England tried to hold the line with foreign- exchange intervention and emergency rate rises. Markets still judged the peg unsustainable. By the evening, the UK had left the ERM. The immediate political damage was severe because the policy had been defended as a badge of competence. When the anchor broke, it was not only the pound that moved. The government’s economic credibility moved with it.

That does not mean the exit was economically disastrous forever. One reason Black Wednesday remains complicated is that sterling’s break also restored monetary flexibility. Once the UK was no longer defending the peg, policy could be set around domestic conditions rather than a fixed exchange-rate commitment. The humiliation and the recovery both matter. They show that the cost of leaving a bad regime can be lower than the cost of pretending it is still viable.

The relevance to today’s MPC is the difference between credibility and inflexibility. If the Bank shrugs off an energy shock, inflation expectations can loosen. But if it clings to a rate path after demand and labour data have softened, it risks mistaking toughness for trust. Black Wednesday’s warning is that markets eventually test whether the official framework still matches the economy beneath it.

The better historical lesson is not “never defend credibility”. It is “choose the anchor carefully”. A central bank can defend the inflation target, but it should not defend an unnecessary degree of restrictiveness simply because changing course looks awkward. The most credible regime is the one that can explain why it holds, why it cuts, and what new evidence would make it change its mind.
