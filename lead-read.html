Higher-for-longer is really a discipline story.

Recent official guidance from the IMF, ECB, and Bank of England points in the same direction: the new energy shock has made inflation less comfortable and growth less secure. Markets are not only repricing the path of rates. They are repricing which businesses and balance sheets deserve patience.

IMF WEO ECB Bank of England
Policy credibility, inflation persistence, and selective resilience.

One easy reading of the moment is that rates may simply stay high for longer because the world has been hit by another inflationary energy shock. That is true, but incomplete. The more important issue is that policymakers are now defending credibility in a setting where inflation expectations could become more sensitive again. When central banks talk about being data-dependent, they are also saying that they do not want to look casual about a new cost shock.

The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook sketches the problem clearly: a weaker growth path paired with a modest renewed rise in inflation, plus the risk of worse outcomes if the shock broadens. The ECB’s March decision and the Bank of England’s March minutes land in a similar place. Neither institution can change global energy prices, but both are alert to the danger that higher costs could seep into wider price-setting behaviour. That keeps the market focused not just on the next policy meeting, but on whether inflation remains well anchored.

For investors, this means “higher-for-longer” is not just about terminal rates. It is a filter. Firms that need constant refinancing, rely on optimistic multiple expansion, or still have not proved their margin durability look more exposed. Companies with better balance sheets, stronger cash conversion, and real pricing power look more investable because they can survive uncertainty without begging for easier policy.

That is why resilience is being priced more selectively. The macro story has shifted from “when do cuts arrive?” towards “which assets can carry themselves if relief arrives late?” In this market, credibility belongs to central banks, but valuation discipline belongs to everyone else.
