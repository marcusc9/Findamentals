Where your next pound should go.

The useful personal-finance question is not which wrapper is fashionable. It is what the next pound is supposed to fix: fragility, expensive debt, missed employer money, tax drag, home deposit value, or long-term compounding.

Order matters because each wrapper solves a different problem.

Start with the job of the pound. If it protects you from forced borrowing, it belongs in emergency cash. If it removes expensive debt, its return is the interest rate you no longer pay. If it unlocks an employer pension match, the uplift can be too large to ignore. Only after those basics should the ISA-versus-taxable account question dominate.

A practical order for many UK readers is: minimum bills and emergency cash first, then high-interest debt, then employer pension match, then ISA capacity, then Lifetime ISA where the home-or-retirement restrictions genuinely fit, then pension or SIPP top-ups, and finally taxable investing once the tax shelters are doing their work. That is a framework, not personal advice.

The formula box is simple. After-tax return equals gross return multiplied by one minus the tax rate. Real return equals one plus the nominal return divided by one plus inflation, minus one. An ISA matters because interest, income, and capital gains inside the wrapper are not taxed. In a taxable account, the same investment may need a higher gross return to produce the same result after tax.

The Lifetime ISA has a different equation. A pound contributed can become £1.25 after the 25 per cent government bonus, subject to the annual LISA limit and the product rules. But access matters: unauthorised withdrawals face a 25 per cent charge on the pot, so the bonus is not free money if you may need the cash for something else. Liquidity is part of the return calculation.

A useful final check is the breakeven. Cash wins when its certain after-tax, inflation- adjusted return is worth more than the uncertainty of investing over the relevant horizon. Investing becomes more compelling when the time horizon is long enough, the emergency fund is already intact, and the reader can tolerate volatility without selling at the wrong time. The next pound should go where it improves the whole system, not just where it looks clever.
