Markets want proof of buffers, not promises.

The latest official macro signals point to a harder test than a simple higher-for-longer call: a Middle East energy shock has lifted inflation risk just as growth confidence has weakened. That makes policy credibility, fiscal headroom, and company balance-sheet resilience the same story told at three different speeds.

IMF WEO ECB Bank of England
Inflation shocks expose which buffers are real.

The market habit is to translate every central-bank signal into one question: when do cuts arrive? That is too narrow for this week. The IMF’s April 2026 World Economic Outlook says global growth is expected to slow to 3.1 per cent in 2026 while headline inflation rises modestly before resuming its decline in 2027. The reason is not a tidy demand cycle. It is a geopolitical shock that threatens commodity prices, expectations, and financial conditions at the same time.

The ECB and the Bank of England are saying the same thing in institutional language. Both can look through part of an energy shock if it fades quickly. Neither can look relaxed if higher fuel, utility, and business-input costs start changing wage and price-setting behaviour. That is why policy credibility now depends less on sounding tough and more on explaining the reaction function: what would make officials hold, what would make them cut, and what would make them lean harder against inflation persistence.

For investors, the practical read-through is discipline. If the cost of capital is no longer reliably falling, businesses have to earn patience. Strong cash conversion, low refinancing pressure, and pricing power matter more. Firms whose equity case depends on cheap funding, heroic terminal multiples, or margin recovery without proof will have less room for excuses.

The same filter applies to governments. Higher defence spending, energy support, and debt servicing costs all compete for fiscal space. When shocks arrive, markets do not only ask whether policymakers have a plan. They ask whether the plan can survive funding pressure. Credibility is therefore not a speech. It is a balance sheet, a policy rule, and a margin of safety.
