Why has private credit gone from quiet portfolio hero to market worry?

Private credit promised patient capital, floating-rate income, and lender discipline. The worry now is that the same structure can make stress slower to see when growth, opacity, leverage, and looser documentation meet a tougher refinancing cycle.

The private-credit story now has to answer like a credit cycle.

The clean way to read negative sentiment around private credit is to avoid the easy bubble label and ask what has changed in the credit plumbing. Borrowers used private credit because it could be faster, more flexible, and less public than syndicated loans or high-yield bonds. Lenders liked directly negotiated, senior secured, floating-rate loans that could be held to maturity rather than marked every second in a public market.

That structure is why the asset class grew. It is also why sentiment can turn. Jamie Dimon's warnings give the concern a clear shape: weak underwriting, aggressive add-backs, covenant erosion, PIK interest, private ratings, opaque marks, and refinancing pressure are not abstract terms. They are the places where a loan can look current while risk is quietly compounding.

The first worry is visibility. Private credit marks can lag reality because loans are less frequently traded and valuations rely more heavily on models, comparables, and manager judgement. That can dampen volatility in normal times, but it also means a portfolio can look steadier than the refinancing market underneath it.

The second worry is liquidity. Semi-liquid funds can give end investors a smoother ride until many people ask for cash at the same time. The loans may be long-dated and hard to sell, while the fund wrapper may create an expectation of access that is only conditional. That is not a bank run, but it is still a mismatch worth pricing.

The third worry is interconnection. The loans may sit outside banks, but banks can still be exposed through lending facilities, warehouse lines, fund finance, advisory relationships, and the broader credit cycle. Private credit is not automatically the next systemic crisis. It does, however, deserve negative sentiment when rapid growth meets weaker documentation, valuation opacity, retail distribution, and a slower refinancing market.
